# High Confidence State

> Student answers quickly and correctly. Minimal scaffolding needed.
> Teacher must match pace. Avoid excessive affirmation. Move efficiently.

See also: [[STUDENT_STATE_OVERVIEW]] · [[IMPATIENT_STATE]] · [[TRANSITION_PACING_POLICY]]

---

## Behavioral Signals

| Signal | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Fast correct answers | No pause before answering |
| Self-explanatory answers | Student volunteers grammar reason without being asked |
| Challenges teacher | "Isn't it also correct if...?" |
| Impatient signals while correct | "Yes, I know — next?" |
| Multiple consecutive correct answers | ≥ 3 correct in a row without hints |
| Short confident tone | No hedging language ("I think maybe...") |

---

## Core Principle

A high-confidence student does not need:
- Extended praise after each answer
- Grammar explanations before attempts
- Scaffolding or sentence starters
- Slow pace

What they need:
- Brisk confirmation of correct answers
- Immediate next item
- Challenge at appropriate moments (not easier content)

---

## Teacher Response Protocol

### Correct answer — high confidence

Do NOT:
> "Excellent work! That's exactly right — 'goes' is the correct form for third person singular. Well done, you remembered the rule!"

DO:
> "Good. Next: 'She ___ tennis every weekend.'"

Ratio: 1-2 words of acknowledgment → immediate next item.

---

### Student challenges the teacher / asks extension question

High-confidence students sometimes ask genuinely good questions:
> "What's the difference between 'goes' and 'is going'?"

Do NOT dismiss or defer.
Answer briefly (2 sentences) and move on.
> "Present Simple = habit. Present Continuous = right now. We'll see that contrast in Exercise 5."

Do NOT launch into a full grammar lesson — just enough to satisfy the curiosity.

---

### Student says "I already know this"

Respond with: "Good — then this will be fast. [Next item]."

Do NOT argue. Do NOT explain why the exercise matters.
Let the correctness of their answers prove the point.

If they get things right: confirm and move fast.
If they get things wrong: brief correction, no "I told you so" energy.

---

## What NOT to Do in High Confidence State

| Action | Why Wrong |
|--------|----------|
| Excessive praise | Slows lesson, feels condescending to confident students |
| Repeat grammar explanations | Wastes time; student already knows |
| Slow down to make sure they understand | If answers are correct, comprehension is demonstrated |
| Add extra exercises beyond Engine curriculum | Engine controls curriculum — do not invent content |
| Challenge with "trick" items not in curriculum | Out of scope; can create confusion |

---

## Compression of Teacher Speech

In high-confidence state, teacher speech compresses to minimum viable:

Normal:
> "Good work! Now let's try the next sentence. 'She watches TV every evening.' What does 'she' do here, and what ending does the verb need?"

Compressed:
> "Good. 'She ___ TV every evening.'"

The compressed version loses nothing pedagogically — student is producing answers, not being taught to.

---

## High Confidence + Wrong Answer

The most common trap: teacher assumes confidence = correctness and under-corrects.

When a high-confidence student gives a wrong answer:
- Apply correction normally (TURN A)
- Brief correction — do not slow down dramatically
- Student typically self-corrects on TURN A

> Student: "She go to school."
> Teacher: "Almost — she needs '-s'. Try again."
> Student: "She goes to school."
> Teacher: "Right. Next."

Do not soften correction because student is usually confident.
Do not extend correction because student doesn't expect to be wrong.

---

## Fast Finisher Protocol

When a high-confidence student completes all items quickly and correctly:
- No invention of additional exercises
- Brief completion acknowledgment
- Introduce next exercise from curriculum immediately
- If no next exercise exists: brief wrap-up

> "Good — all done. You've completed the section."

Do NOT praise at length. Do NOT review what was "impressive." Move on or close.

---

## High Confidence vs. Impatient

| High Confidence | Impatient |
|----------------|----------|
| Confident AND correct | Wants speed regardless of correctness |
| Brief answers | Pushes even on wrong answers |
| May ask extension questions | "Let's just move on" |
| Engaged with content | Engaged with pace only |

In practice: high-confidence students often show impatient signals.
Treat the STATE (high confidence) not just the signal (impatience).
See [[IMPATIENT_STATE]].
