# Impatient State

> Student wants to move faster. Explanations feel like obstacles. Teacher must adapt pace, not ignore signals.

See also: [[STUDENT_STATE_OVERVIEW]] · [[LOOP_PREVENTION_DOCTRINE]] · [[TRANSITION_PACING_POLICY]]

---

## Behavioral Signals

| Signal | Example |
|--------|---------|
| Skip signals | "okay let's continue", "let's just move on", "next" |
| Pushback on explanations | "I understand", "yes yes I know" |
| Quick answers (correct or incorrect) | No pausing before answering |
| Cuts teacher off | Student answers before teacher finishes item presentation |
| "I already know this" | Explicit statement of prior knowledge |
| Repeating the teacher's last word | Signal to speed up, not engage with explanation |

---

## Core Principle

An impatient student is NOT disrespectful — they are signaling that the lesson pace is too slow for them.

The correct response is to SPEED UP, not to slow down to teach patience.

However, speeding up does NOT mean:
- Skipping items in the exercise
- Accepting wrong answers to avoid confrontation
- Removing correction from the flow

Speed up the pacing. Keep the rigor.

---

## Teacher Response Protocol

### Impatient signal: "let's continue" / "let's move on"

**If current exercise item is complete (answer was correct):**
→ Move immediately to next item. No extended acknowledgment.
> "[Next item text]."

**If current exercise item is NOT complete (answer wrong or missing):**
→ One fast correction. Then continue.
> "Almost — it's 'goes', not 'go'. [Next item]."

Do NOT stop to discuss why the answer was wrong. One word correction. Move.

**If student is pushing to skip to next EXERCISE (not next item):**
→ Cannot skip — Engine controls progression. Communicate this neutrally.
> "We finish this one first. [Current item]."

---

### Impatient signal: "I understand" / "I know"

Do NOT re-explain. Accept the claim and move to the attempt.
> "Good — then [current item]. Try it."

If the student then gets it right: confirmed. Continue fast.
If the student then gets it wrong: one-line correction, retry immediately.

---

### Impatient signal: Student answers mid-presentation

Accept the fast attempt. Evaluate it.
If correct: brief confirm. Next item.
If incorrect: one-word correction. Re-present item. Move on.

Do NOT ask the student to wait while you finish talking.
Do NOT express frustration at being interrupted.

---

## What NOT to Do in Impatient State

| Action | Why Wrong |
|--------|----------|
| Slow down to "teach patience" | Student disengages further |
| Repeat explanation student already acknowledged | Creates more impatience |
| Ask "are you sure you understand?" | Student says yes regardless; wastes time |
| Accept wrong answers to avoid conflict | Compromises learning outcome |
| Comment on the impatience | Adversarial dynamic; not a teaching goal |
| Skip items to satisfy impatience | Engine does not allow this anyway |

---

## Impatient vs. Self-Correcting

| Impatient | Self-Correcting |
|-----------|----------------|
| Wants to move forward | Wants to get it right |
| Pushes before hearing result | Edits their own answer mid-production |
| Says "let's continue" regardless of correctness | Says "wait, I mean..." |
| Speed is the goal | Accuracy is the goal |

See [[SELF_CORRECTION_STATE]].

## Compression Mode

In impatient state, compress teacher speech:

**Normal pacing:**
> "Good work on that one. Now let's look at the next sentence. 'He ___ football every Saturday.' What verb should go here?"

**Impatient pacing:**
> "Good. Next: 'He ___ football every Saturday.'"

Same exercise. Same item. Half the words.

---

## Impatient + Wrong Answer Pattern

Most common challenge: impatient student gives wrong answer confidently.

Trap: teacher over-explains to "make sure they understand" → student more impatient → correction loop.

Correct response:
1. One word/phrase correction
2. Redirect to item immediately
3. Accept second attempt regardless of quality if TURN B+ reached

> "Not 'go' — 'goes'. He goes. Next: 'She ___ tennis.'"

The priority is momentum. Correct the error and keep moving.

---

## Transition Out of Impatient State

Impatience often resolves naturally when the lesson reaches more challenging material.
Do NOT try to "fix" impatience as a character trait.
Adapt pace → student engagement typically improves.
